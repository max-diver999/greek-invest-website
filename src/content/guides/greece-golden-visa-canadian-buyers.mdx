## Tax, AFM, and Bank Setup for Canadian Buyers

Every Canadian property purchase in Greece begins with administrative infrastructure, not property selection. The AFM, Greece's tax identification number, is mandatory before transfer tax payment and notarial deed signing. Canadian buyers obtain it at a DOY tax office with a valid passport or remotely via a Greek lawyer holding a notarised power of attorney, typically within one to fourteen business days depending on route.

The AFM connects to every downstream obligation: the 3.09% property transfer tax on resale purchases, the E9 annual property declaration filed within sixty days of acquisition, and ENFIA, the annual property ownership tax calculated on objective assessed value. Canadian non-residents receive a Pink Slip confirming fiscal residence outside Greece and naming a fiscal representative, usually their lawyer.

Golden Visa applicants face an additional requirement under Circular 1/2026: a Greek bank account through which the full purchase price must be traceable. This is not optional for permit purposes. The bank will require passport, AFM, proof of funds origin, and increasingly rigorous KYC documentation for non-EU nationals. Canadian buyers should budget two to four weeks for account opening and expect their lawyer to coordinate the sequence with wire transfer timelines from Canadian banks.

## Canadian Tax Obligations: T1135, Foreign Income, and Treaty Credits

Canadian tax residents purchasing Greek property face two parallel reporting regimes: Greek domestic obligations and Canadian cross-border disclosure requirements. Both apply regardless of whether the property generates income.

**Form T1135 (Foreign Income Verification Statement)** is mandatory for Canadian residents holding specified foreign property with a total cost amount exceeding CAD 100,000 at any time during the tax year. A €400,000 Crete purchase at 1.45 CAD/EUR exchange rate equals approximately CAD 580,000, well above the threshold. T1135 must be filed annually with the Canadian tax return. Failure to file carries penalties starting at CAD 25 per day, up to CAD 2,500, plus potential prosecution for wilful non-disclosure.

**Greek rental income** is taxable in Greece first under Greek domestic law. Greece taxes rental income progressively starting at 15% on amounts up to €12,000 annually, rising to 45% on income exceeding €35,000. Canadian residents must then report the same income on their Canadian tax return but can claim a foreign tax credit under Article 23 of the Canada-Greece Income Tax Convention to avoid double taxation. The credit mechanism generally prevents paying tax twice on the same income, but timing mismatches and calculation complexity mean buyers should consult cross-border tax advisers before assuming full offset.

**Capital gains on sale** are taxable in Greece at 15% on gains exceeding €25,000 cumulative. Canadian residents report the gain on their Canadian return, again with foreign tax credit mechanisms under the treaty. Exchange rate fluctuations between purchase, rental income periods, and eventual sale add complexity: a property bought at 1.40 CAD/EUR and sold at 1.50 CAD/EUR may show a CAD gain even if euro price remains flat.

| --- | --- | --- | --- |

| Greek rental income | Greece AADE | First euro | Greek tax first, then CRA with credit |

ENFIA, Greece's annual property ownership tax, is calculated on objective assessed value and varies by location, size, and age. A €400,000 Crete property might carry €800 to €1,500 ENFIA annually; an €800,000 Riviera apartment might run €2,000 to €4,000. ENFIA is not creditable against Canadian income tax, it is a wealth tax on the asset itself.

Canadian buyers should engage a cross-border tax adviser familiar with Greece before purchase to model total tax burden, T1135 mechanics, and treaty credit sequencing. The Canada Revenue Agency publishes guidance on Form T1135 and foreign property reporting; non-compliance audits have increased significantly since 2015.

## Due Diligence: What Canadian Buyers Must Verify Before Deposit

Canadian buyers often arrive with strong deal instincts from competitive Toronto and Vancouver markets, but Greek property due diligence follows a different legal architecture. The notary authenticates the deed; the notary does not protect the buyer. Title verification, cadastre status, encumbrance checks, and Golden Visa eligibility confirmation are the lawyer's job, and skipping this step to save €3,000 to €5,000 in fees has cost buyers multiples of that when defects surfaced post-purchase.

The non-negotiable checklist before any deposit:

1. **Cadastre extract**: confirms ownership, boundaries, and encumbrances on the Hellenic National Cadastre (Ktimatologio)
2. **Usable area verification**: marketing m² often includes terraces and parking; only cadastral κύριος χώρος counts toward the 120 m² Golden Visa rule
3. **Building permit compliance**: unauthorised extensions are common in older Attica and island stock
4. **ENFIA objective value**: determines transfer tax base and annual holding cost
5. **Mortgage and lien search**: confirms no undisclosed encumbrances
6. **Single-title confirmation**: two apartments on one deed may still fail the 120 m² usable-area test if combined areas include non-qualifying space
7. **Exchange rate lock strategy**: CAD/EUR volatility means a €400,000 property can swing by CAD 20,000+ between offer and closing

Canadian buyers purchasing remotely, common given Toronto-Athens or Vancouver-Athens flight schedules, should execute the entire due diligence phase through a lawyer with a notarised power of attorney before travelling for the notary signing. Never wire a deposit based on portal photographs and agent assurances alone.

For buyers coordinating wire transfers from Canadian banks, confirm SWIFT timelines and intermediary bank fees. Canadian banks typically require three to five business days for international wires; Greek notaries expect confirmed cleared funds before signing. Budget an extra week in the timeline for banking friction.

## Short-Term Rental Ban: What Canadians Must Understand

Law 5100/2024 prohibits short-term tourist rentals on the qualifying Golden Visa property. This is not a grey area. Operating Airbnb, Booking.com, or similar platforms on the GV asset risks fines under Greek tourism law and jeopardises permit renewal.

Canadian buyers accustomed to Toronto Airbnb economics or Vancouver short-stay rental markets sometimes receive agent pitches positioning Golden Visa apartments as high-yield STR assets. That framing is misleading for the qualifying property. The permitted income model is long-term residential lease only, typically twelve-month contracts to professionals, students, or families.

Investors who want both Golden Visa residency and tourism rental income need a two-asset structure: one GV-qualifying property on LTR, and a separate non-GV property (often in Crete or an island market) licensed for short-term rental. Budget and tax implications double, but regulatory constraints stay clean. T1135 reporting requirements apply to both properties if total foreign holdings exceed CAD 100,000.

## Why Canadian Buyers Need a Greek Lawyer: Not Just a Notary

Greek property law separates the notary's role from buyer representation. The notary is a neutral public officer who ensures the deed is legally valid. The notary does not search title, verify cadastre, challenge the preliminary agreement, or confirm Golden Visa eligibility.

Canadian buyers, especially those purchasing remotely from Toronto, Vancouver, or Montreal, need a buyer-side lawyer who:

- Obtains the AFM and coordinates Pink Slip issuance
- Conducts full title and cadastre due diligence
- Verifies 120 m² usable area against Ministry of Migration requirements
- Reviews or drafts the preliminary agreement (προσύμφωνο) before deposit
- Calculates transfer tax and coordinates payment timing with Canadian wire schedules
- Represents at the notary signing via power of attorney if needed
- Submits the Golden Visa application after deed registration

Legal fees run 1.0 to 2.0% of the purchase price. On an €800,000 Riviera purchase, that is €8,000 to €16,000, modest relative to the cost of discovering a title defect, a sub-120 m² cadastral area, or an unauthorised building extension after completion. For remote Canadian buyers coordinating across time zones, the lawyer also functions as the primary communication bridge with sellers, agents, banks, and tax offices.

## Risks Canadian Buyers Should Price Before Signing

**Sub-120 m² cadastral risk** is the most common Golden Visa disqualifier. Marketing floor plans routinely overstate usable area. Mitigation: lawyer obtains engineer-certified cadastral extract before deposit.

**STR assumption risk** leads buyers to model Airbnb yields on the GV asset. Mitigation: model LTR only on qualifying property; budget separately for STR on non-GV stock.

**Remote purchase risk** without power of attorney exposes buyers to signing preliminary agreements they have not had counsel review. Mitigation: appoint lawyer before property search, not after offer. Toronto and Vancouver buyers should budget for notarised power of attorney at Canadian notary public before initiating due diligence.

**Exchange rate risk** on CAD/EUR conversion at offer stage. A €400,000 property can swing by CAD 20,000 to 30,000 between offer and closing if CAD weakens from 1.45 to 1.50 EUR. Mitigation: lock FX strategy with adviser or use forward contracts; model total euro cost including 7 to 10% acquisition stack before committing.

**Permit timeline risk**: buyers planning immediate Schengen reliance may face 8 to 14 months between deed and permit. Mitigation: maintain existing Canadian passport and visa-free 90/180 access during processing; do not cancel Toronto or Vancouver lease or school commitments based on purchase alone.

**T1135 penalty risk**: CRA audits of foreign property non-disclosure have increased significantly. Penalties start at CAD 25/day. Mitigation: engage cross-border tax adviser before purchase; file T1135 annually even if property generates no income.

**Inheritance complexity**: Greek inheritance law and tax differ from Canadian common-law structures. Canadian wills may not transfer cleanly to Greek property. Mitigation: draft Greek will simultaneously with purchase; consult estate planner familiar with Greece before structuring title.

## Common Mistakes Canadian Buyers Make

**Buying below 120 m² assuming aggregation.** Law 5100/2024 requires a single property. Two 70 m² apartments do not qualify regardless of combined value.

**Trusting agent Golden Visa guarantees.** Only the Ministry of Migration issues permits. Agents cannot confirm eligibility; lawyers verify cadastral area and zone classification.

**Skipping engineer certificate on resale stock.** Older Attica buildings frequently carry unauthorised extensions that block clean resale and complicate GV files.

**Depositing before cadastre check.** Greek law allows preliminary agreements with 10% deposits; Canadian buyers sometimes wire from Toronto or Vancouver banks before title search completes. Reversing a bad deal after deposit is expensive and slow across jurisdictions.

**Ignoring T1135 at purchase stage.** CRA expects annual filing for holdings over CAD 100,000. First-year non-compliance carries penalties. Model compliance cost and complexity before purchase, not after deed.

**Using the GV property for family short stays marketed online.** Even occasional Airbnb bookings on the qualifying asset violate permit conditions. Personal use is fine; commercial short-stay platforms are not.

**Assuming Canadian mortgage portability.** Canadian residential mortgages cannot be ported to Greek property. Greek mortgage rates for non-EU buyers run 4.0 to 6.0% as of 2026, higher than Canadian prime. Most Canadian buyers purchase all-cash or leverage Toronto/Vancouver equity via HELOC before transfer.
