Greek inheritance tax is not one flat percentage on the entire estate. Heirs are grouped into relationship categories, historically described as Category I, II, and III in practitioner language, with Category I covering the closest family lines and Category III covering distant or non-related beneficiaries. Each category carries a tax-free allowance applied to the heir's share, then progressive marginal rates on the amount above that allowance.

Because finance ministry decisions adjust euro thresholds and rate schedules, any number you see online may be stale. Licensed counsel typically models three scenarios: spouse and children, siblings or more remote blood relatives, and unrelated heirs or distant cousins. The pattern is stable even when digits move: closer relatives pay less on the same apartment value than distant heirs.

| Heir relationship (typical grouping) | Practitioner description | Allowance tendency | Rate tendency on taxable remainder |

| Grandchildren, siblings, in-laws in some cases | Middle category | Moderate allowance | Mid-range progressive bands |
| Distant kin, unrelated beneficiaries | Broadest category | Smallest or zero allowance | Highest bands, can reach high single digits or low double digits on large shares |

## Will vs Intestacy: Which Path Applies to Foreign Owners?

Greek succession for foreign owners typically requires choosing among a home-country will, a Greek notarial will for local assets, or Greek intestacy rules that default shares to spouse and children under statutory order, with acceptance declarations due within 4 months of death for heirs present in Greece under current practitioner timelines.

Three documents often collide in foreign-owner estates: a home-country will, a Greek notarial will limited to local assets, and Greek intestacy rules if no valid will covers the property.

**Greek notarial will:** Drafted before a Greek notary, stored in the notarial archive, directly enforceable for Greek assets. Preferred when the owner spends significant time in Greece or holds multiple local properties.

**Foreign will:** Valid if it meets form requirements, but translation, apostille, and conflict-of-law analysis add months. Greek notaries may hesitate if forced-heirship produces a different result than the foreign document.

**Intestacy:** If no valid will governs the Greek asset, Greek statutory order determines heirs, typically spouse and children in defined shares, then parents, then more remote lines. Intestacy does not avoid tax; it merely defaults the split.

|---|---|---|

| Single home-country will | Convenient while living abroad | Slower probate, conflict risk on immovable |
| Living owners holding joint structure | Can simplify transfer on first death | Tax and GV rules may not favour joint for non-spouses |
| Lifetime gift instead of testament | May shift timing of tax event | Gift tax applies; GV minimum hold rules if permit active |
