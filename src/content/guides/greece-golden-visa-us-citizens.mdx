## Schengen 90/180 Limit: How Golden Visa Solves the American Tourist Constraint

US passport holders enter the Schengen zone visa-free for tourism, but face a hard limit of ninety days in any rolling 180-day period across all twenty-seven Schengen member states. For Americans working remotely in Europe, maintaining second homes, or spending extended time across multiple countries, this constraint becomes the binding residency bottleneck.

The Greece Golden Visa eliminates the tourist limit by granting legal residency status. Once the five-year residency permit is issued, the holder is no longer subject to the ninety-day cap within Greece and benefits from extended-stay provisions across the broader Schengen zone under long-stay residence rules, though precise multi-country rules vary by member state. In practice, Americans with Greek Golden Visa residency can remain in Greece indefinitely and travel freely across Schengen for short trips without counting days.

The zero minimum physical presence requirement is critical for US citizens. Unlike residency programs that mandate 183 days per year or conditional presence tied to employment, Greece Golden Visa requires no minimum stay in any calendar year. Americans can hold the permit purely for optionality, renew it every five years by maintaining the property and paying modest administrative fees, and never reside in Greece if they choose. The residency card remains valid regardless of actual time spent on Greek soil.

## FATCA and IRS Reporting: What US Golden Visa Holders Must Disclose

US citizens are subject to worldwide income taxation and extensive foreign asset reporting regardless of residency. Greece Golden Visa participation does not change citizenship-based tax obligations, but it creates new reporting layers that American buyers must navigate with cross-border tax counsel.

**FBAR (FinCEN Form 114):** US persons with foreign financial accounts exceeding an aggregate ten thousand dollars at any point during the calendar year must file an annual Report of Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts. The Greek bank account required under Circular 1/2026 for Golden Visa applicants is a reportable account. Failure to file FBAR carries steep penalties, including potential criminal charges for willful violations.

**FATCA (Form 8938):** US taxpayers must report specified foreign financial assets on Form 8938 if total value exceeds threshold amounts, generally fifty thousand dollars for single filers living in the US or two hundred thousand dollars for those living abroad. The Greek bank account holding property transaction funds and subsequent rental income deposits qualifies. Real property itself, the apartment or villa deed, is not a reportable financial asset under FATCA, but any financial account linked to that property is.

**Greek rental income:** Long-term rental income from the Golden Visa property is taxable in both Greece and the US. Greece taxes non-resident rental income at progressive rates starting at fifteen percent on net taxable income. The US taxes worldwide income, but allows foreign tax credits for Greek taxes paid. Americans must file both Greek annual property declarations (E9) and report the same income on US Form 1040 Schedule E.

**No estate or gift tax treaty:** Greece and the US lack an estate tax treaty. Americans holding Greek property at death may face Greek inheritance tax (rates vary by heir relationship, ranging from one to forty percent) and US estate tax if total worldwide estate exceeds the federal exemption threshold, currently over thirteen million dollars per individual in 2026. Proper estate planning with both Greek and US counsel is essential for high-net-worth buyers.

**Exit tax considerations:** Americans considering eventual renunciation of US citizenship face exit tax under IRC Section 877A if net worth exceeds two million dollars or average annual net income tax exceeds a threshold amount. The Greece Golden Visa property and any appreciation are included in mark-to-market calculations. This is a niche concern, but relevant for Americans exploring long-term emigration scenarios.

Most US Golden Visa buyers work with cross-border tax advisers who prepare both Greek E9 declarations and US tax filings, coordinate foreign tax credit claims, and ensure FBAR and FATCA compliance. Budget two thousand to five thousand dollars annually for dual-jurisdiction tax preparation if rental income is present.

## Tax, AFM, and Greek Bank Account Setup for US Buyers

Every US property purchase in Greece begins with administrative infrastructure, not property selection. The AFM, Greece's tax identification number, is mandatory before transfer tax payment and notarial deed signing. US buyers obtain it at a DOY tax office with a valid passport or remotely via a Greek lawyer holding a notarised power of attorney, typically within one to fourteen business days depending on route.

The AFM connects to every downstream obligation: the 3.09 percent property transfer tax on resale purchases, the E9 annual property declaration filed within sixty days of acquisition, and ENFIA, the annual property ownership tax calculated on objective assessed value. US non-residents receive a Pink Slip confirming fiscal residence outside Greece and naming a fiscal representative, usually their Greek lawyer.

Golden Visa applicants face an additional requirement under Circular 1/2026: a Greek bank account through which the full purchase price must be traceable. This is not optional for permit purposes. The bank will require passport, AFM, proof of funds origin, and increasingly rigorous KYC documentation for non-EU nationals including Americans. US buyers should budget two to four weeks for account opening, expect FATCA disclosure forms, and coordinate the sequence with their lawyer to ensure the bank account is operational before deposit.

## Due Diligence: What US Buyers Must Verify Before Deposit

American buyers often arrive with strong real estate diligence instincts from US markets, but Greek property due diligence follows a different legal architecture. The notary authenticates the deed; the notary does not protect the buyer. Title verification, cadastre status, encumbrance checks, and Golden Visa eligibility confirmation are the lawyer's job, and skipping this step to save €3,000–€5,000 in fees has cost buyers multiples of that when defects surfaced post-purchase.

The non-negotiable checklist before any deposit:

1. **Cadastre extract**: confirms ownership, boundaries, and encumbrances on the Hellenic National Cadastre (Ktimatologio)
2. **Usable area verification**: marketing square metres often include terraces, parking, and storage; only cadastral κύριος χώρος (principal area) counts toward the 120 m² Golden Visa rule
3. **Building permit compliance**: unauthorised extensions are common in older stock, particularly on islands and Attica
4. **ENFIA objective value**: determines transfer tax base and annual holding cost
5. **Border zone status**: non-EU buyers including Americans need Ministry of Defense clearance in restricted eastern Aegean islands near Turkey; Athens, Crete, and most mainland regions are generally clear
6. **Mortgage and lien search**: confirms no undisclosed encumbrances
7. **Single-title confirmation**: two apartments on one deed may still fail the 120 m² usable-area test if combined areas include non-qualifying balcony or basement space

US buyers purchasing remotely, common given transatlantic flight logistics, should execute the entire due diligence phase through a Greek lawyer with a notarised power of attorney before travelling for the notary signing. Never wire a deposit based on portal photographs, agent assurances, or virtual tours alone. Greek property law does not provide the same buyer protections as US title insurance structures; the lawyer is the only intermediary protecting your capital.

## Short-Term Rental Ban: What American Buyers Must Understand

Law 5100/2024 prohibits short-term tourist rentals on the qualifying Golden Visa property. This is not a grey area or a selectively enforced rule. Operating Airbnb, VRBO, Booking.com, or similar platforms on the GV asset risks fines under Greek tourism law and jeopardises permit renewal.

American buyers accustomed to US short-term rental economics, particularly in Florida, California, or Colorado mountain markets, sometimes receive agent or developer pitches positioning Golden Visa apartments as high-yield STR assets. That framing is misleading for the qualifying property. The permitted income model is long-term residential lease only, typically twelve-month contracts to local professionals, students, expat families, or seasonal workers.

Investors who want both Golden Visa residency and tourism rental income need a two-asset structure: one GV-qualifying property on long-term rental generating 4.0–6.0 percent gross, and a separate non-GV property, often in a Crete coastal village or an island market, licensed for short-term rental generating 8.0–12.0 percent gross in high season. Budget and tax implications double, but regulatory constraints stay clean. US buyers pursuing this route should model total capital deployment and tax filing complexity before committing.

## Why US Buyers Need a Greek Lawyer: Not Just a Notary

Greek property law separates the notary's role from buyer representation. The notary is a neutral public officer who ensures the deed is legally valid and authentic. The notary does not search title, verify cadastre, challenge the preliminary agreement, or confirm Golden Visa eligibility. That separation is unfamiliar to Americans accustomed to US title insurance and closing attorney structures where buyer interests are explicitly protected.

US buyers, especially those purchasing remotely from the United States, need a buyer-side Greek lawyer who:

- Obtains the AFM and coordinates Pink Slip issuance
- Conducts full title and cadastre due diligence
- Verifies 120 m² usable area against Ministry of Migration requirements
- Reviews or drafts the preliminary agreement (προσύμφωνο) before deposit
- Calculates transfer tax and coordinates payment timing before notary
- Represents at the notary signing via power of attorney if the buyer cannot travel
- Submits the Golden Visa application after deed registration with all supporting documents

Legal fees run 1.0–2.0 percent of the purchase price. On a €400,000 Crete purchase, that is €4,000–€8,000, modest relative to the cost of discovering a title defect, a sub-120 m² cadastral area, or an unauthorised building extension after completion. The lawyer is the only party in the transaction whose fiduciary duty is to protect the buyer's capital and legal interests.

## Common Mistakes US Buyers Make

**Buying below 120 m² assuming aggregation works.** Law 5100/2024 requires a single property. Two 70 m² apartments do not qualify regardless of combined value or ownership under one deed.

**Trusting agent or developer Golden Visa guarantees.** Only the Ministry of Migration issues permits. Agents and developers cannot confirm eligibility; Greek lawyers verify cadastral area, zone classification, and single-title compliance before deposit.

**Skipping engineer certificate on resale stock.** Older Attica and island buildings frequently carry unauthorised extensions, enclosed balconies, or unpermitted additions that block clean resale and complicate GV application files. Always obtain an engineer certificate during due diligence.

**Depositing before cadastre and title check completes.** Greek law allows preliminary agreements with ten percent deposits; American buyers sometimes wire funds before the lawyer completes full diligence. Reversing a bad deal after deposit is expensive and often requires litigation.

**Ignoring ENFIA at purchase stage.** Annual property tax on an €800,000 Athens property can run €2,000–€5,000 depending on objective assessed value. Model holding costs before yield calculations, particularly if the property will sit vacant while you remain in the US.

**Assuming Golden Visa residency solves US tax obligations.** Greek residency does not change US citizenship-based taxation. Americans remain subject to worldwide income reporting, FBAR, FATCA, and estate tax regardless of where they hold residency cards.

**Using the GV property for occasional Airbnb bookings.** Even sporadic short-stay rentals marketed online violate permit conditions. Personal family use is fine; commercial tourist rental platforms are not. The distinction matters for enforcement and permit renewal.

## Risks US Buyers Should Price Before Signing

**Sub-120 m² cadastral risk** is the most common Golden Visa disqualifier. Marketing floor plans and agent materials routinely overstate usable area by including terraces, balconies, parking, and storage. Mitigation: Greek lawyer obtains engineer-certified cadastral extract confirming κύριος χώρος before deposit, not after.

**STR assumption risk** leads US buyers to model Airbnb yields on the GV asset based on US market comparables. Mitigation: model long-term rental only on qualifying property, typically 4.0–6.0 percent gross; budget separately for STR on non-GV stock if tourism yield is a priority.

**Remote purchase risk** without proper power of attorney and lawyer representation exposes buyers to signing preliminary agreements they have not had counsel review. Mitigation: appoint Greek lawyer before property search begins, not after offer acceptance. Execute notarised POA in the US if you cannot attend notary signing in Greece.

**Athens Riviera overpayment risk** in Glyfada, Voula, and Vouliagmeni where asking prices often exceed recent notarial transaction comparables by fifteen to twenty-five percent. Mitigation: lawyer provides recent comparable deeds for target buildings, not portal listings alone. Negotiate based on registered transaction prices.

**Permit timeline risk** where buyers plan immediate Schengen reliance based on purchase alone. Greece Golden Visa processing takes eight to fourteen months after deed registration due to Ministry of Migration backlog. Mitigation: maintain existing US passport and Schengen tourist allowance during processing; do not cancel US lease, job, or school commitments based on property purchase until residency card is physically issued.

**Currency and exchange rate risk** on dollar-to-euro conversion between offer and final payment. A five percent euro appreciation against the dollar between preliminary agreement and notary deed adds twenty thousand dollars to the cost of a €400,000 purchase. Mitigation: lock FX strategy with financial adviser or use forward contracts before deposit; model total euro cost including 7–10 percent acquisition stack in dollar terms upfront.

**FATCA non-compliance risk** if US buyer fails to report Greek bank account or rental income. Penalties for willful FBAR violations can reach fifty percent of account value per year. Mitigation: engage cross-border tax adviser before purchase; file FinCEN 114 annually and Form 8938 if thresholds are met; report Greek rental income on US and Greek returns.
